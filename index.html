Difficulties with Bayesian inference¶

3 pt(s)

Many realistic problems study not a single parameter, but a whole bunch of them. For example, one might be interested in networks. A network consists of nodes, that can represent people, proteins, brain regions, traffic junctions, etc., and edges, that represent the interactions between nodes. Note that there are many more connections than nodes!

The presence of an interaction between two nodes can again be represented by a Bernoulli variable – the connection is either present or absent. Typically, the individual connections are assumed to be independent a priori. This makes it easier to define a prior over the entire network $A=\{a\}_{ij}$ as the product of the individual connections

$$ p(A\mid \theta) = \prod_{i\neq j} p(a_{ij}\mid \theta) \enspace, $$

where $p(a_{ij}\mid \theta)$ is the familiar Bernoulli likelihood. The difficulty in computing the posterior of a network, i.e. $p(A\mid x,\theta)$, where $x$ are observations, is that if the posterior does not have a closed form solution, the sum in marginal likelihood

$$ p(x\mid \theta) = \sum_{A} p(x\mid A) p(A \mid \theta) $$

can be over many terms; too many to realistically compute.

Assume the network $A$ contains no self-loops (i.e. $a_{ii}=0$ for all $i$), and assume the networks are directed (that is $a_{ij}$ is another connection than $a_{ji}$).

  1. Under what condition is the posterior $p(A\mid x,\theta)$ not in closed form?

Your answer here.

</span>

This is the case when the prior is not a conjugate, so when the prior and posterior do not look the same, which means it is difficult to solve the integral with calculus(not closed).

  1. What would happen if you just skipped the marginal likelihood altogether, i.e. you would compute

    \begin{equation}

     p(A\mid x,\theta) \propto p(x\mid A,\theta) p (A\mid \theta) \enspace.

    \end{equation}

Your answer here.

</span>

The property of normalization is lost, the total sum of the distribution is not 1 anymore. Unnormalized distributions are useful to describe a data distribution. It is easier and more meaningful to model the shape of the data distribution. The properties that are intact are the domain (theta) is still between 0 and 1 and the shape. An application where it is still useful is in neural probabilistic language models, this can be used for machine translation, sentence completion, or speech recognition

Conjugate priors¶

3 pt(s)

When you have a likelihood such as the Bernoulli distribution, i.e. $p(z,N\mid \theta)$, as well as a conjugate prior such as the beta distribution, $p(\theta\mid a,b)$, we might not care about $\theta$ if we only want to model the data adequately (instead of wanting to infer $\theta$). In that case, we can also define

\begin{equation} p(z,N\mid a,b) = \int_0^1 \! \, \overbrace{p(z,N\mid \theta)}^{\mathrm{likelihood}} \underbrace{p(\theta\mid a,b)}_{\mathrm{prior}} \,\mathrm{d}\theta \enspace, \end{equation}

i.e. we have integrated out (or marginalized out) the parameter $\theta$. This means we do not care about $\theta$, but we let the data depend directly on our pseudo counts.

Now integrals can be difficult compute, but being clever goes a long way.

  1. Prove that \begin{equation} p(z,N\mid a,b) = \frac{B(z+a, N-z+b)}{B(a,b)} \end{equation}.

Note: if you are using a lot of calculus to get to the result, you are doing it wrong! You only have to expand the formulae for prior and likelihood and look carefully at their formulas.

Your answer here.

</span>

\begin{equation} p(z,N\mid a,b) = \int_0^1 \! \, \overbrace{p(z,N\mid \theta)}^{\mathrm{likelihood}} \underbrace{p(\theta\mid a,b)}_{\mathrm{prior}} \,\mathrm{d}\theta \enspace, \end{equation}

\begin{equation} p(z, N) = {\theta^{z}(1-\theta)^{N-z}} \end{equation}

.

\begin{equation} p(\theta\mid a,b) = {\theta^{a-1}(1-\theta)^{b-1} \frac{1}{B(a,b)}} \end{equation}
